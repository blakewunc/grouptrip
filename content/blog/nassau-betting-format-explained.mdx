If you've played golf with money on the line, you've probably played Nassau — even if you didn't know it by name. It's the most common group betting format in the game, simple enough to run in your head and engaging enough to keep everyone interested from the first tee to the last putt.

Here's everything you need to know.

---

## What Is Nassau?

Nassau is a three-part bet played over 18 holes:

1. **Front nine** — a match between players or teams for the front 9 holes
2. **Back nine** — a separate match for the back 9 holes
3. **Overall** — a match for the full 18 holes

Each of the three matches has its own stake. In a "$5 Nassau," each match is worth $5. You can win or lose up to $15 (front, back, overall).

The matches are scored hole-by-hole by stroke play (gross or net score). The player/team with the lower score on a hole wins that hole. Whoever wins more holes wins the match.

---

## How It's Scored

Each hole is worth one point in each active match. You're simultaneously playing:
- A hole-by-hole match against your opponent for the front
- A hole-by-hole match against your opponent for the back
- A hole-by-hole match against your opponent for the 18

For example, after 9 holes:
- You won holes 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 — 5 holes
- Your opponent won holes 2, 4, 6, 8 — 4 holes
- You win the front match by 1 up → your opponent owes you $5

Then the back nine starts fresh. Your overall match continues from 9 up.

At the end of 18, you tally the front result, back result, and overall result. Each is paid or collected separately.

---

## Gross vs. Net Nassau

**Gross Nassau:** Scores are compared without handicap strokes. Works well for groups with similar handicaps. The better golfer has a consistent edge.

**Net Nassau:** Each player applies their course handicap (or a negotiated number of strokes) before comparing scores. This levels the field and is standard for mixed-handicap groups.

In a net Nassau, if you're a 12-handicap and your opponent is a 4-handicap, you get 8 strokes distributed across the 18 holes based on handicap stroke index. On a hole with a stroke, your net score is one better than your gross score.

**Recommendation:** For group trips with mixed handicaps, always play net Nassau. Gross Nassau with a 5-handicap against a 22-handicap isn't a bet — it's a donation.

---

## Presses

A press is a side bet that activates within the Nassau. When a player or team falls two down in any of the three matches, they can "press" — opening a new, parallel match for the remaining holes.

Example: You're 2 down on the front nine after hole 5. You press. Now you have:
- The original front nine match (you're 2 down)
- A new press match starting on hole 6 for the remaining 4 holes on the front

If you win the press match, you offset some of your loss on the original front. If you lose it, your debt compounds.

**Automatic press:** Some groups agree to automatic presses (you must press when 2 down). This keeps the bet alive longer and creates more action toward the end.

**Voluntary press:** You can press anytime you want, not just when 2 down. More aggressive players use voluntary presses to increase stakes mid-round.

Presses add complexity but keep losing players engaged. If you're going to play presses, make sure everyone understands the rules before teeing off.

## Setting the Right Stakes

The right Nassau stake depends on your group. A few guidelines:

**Too low ($1 Nassau):** Nobody cares. You can lose $3 and feel nothing. Doesn't add enough tension to be interesting.

**About right ($5–$10 Nassau):** Real money, not scary money. A bad round costs you $15–30, a good round earns you the same. Creates consistent interest throughout.

**High stakes ($25+ Nassau):** Changes how people play. Some love it. Some hate it. Only do this if the whole group is on board and the stakes don't stress anyone out financially.

**Rule of thumb:** The stake should be high enough that a $15 loss stings a little, but not so high that anyone leaves the round angry.

## Team Nassau

Nassau works equally well for teams (2v2 or 4v4) as it does for individual matches.

**Best ball (2v2):** Each team plays their two best scores on each hole. The lower team score wins the hole.

**Scramble format:** Both team members hit, pick the best shot, and play from there. Fun for corporate events; less skill-differentiating for competitive groups.

**Four-ball match play:** Standard best ball, but scored as a match (one point per hole) rather than by total strokes. Common for Ryder Cup-style events.

For a group of 8, you can run a team Nassau with four 2-person teams, or pair everyone up for individual 1v1 matches.

## Nassau vs. Other Formats

**Skins:** Each hole is worth a fixed pot. If nobody wins a hole outright (tie), the pot carries over. Creates big payouts on later holes. More variance than Nassau, less strategic.

**Wolf:** One player is designated "wolf" on each hole. They can pick a partner from the other players (or go alone for a 3x payout). Requires 4 players and rotation. Fun but complex.

**Stableford:** Points-based system (birdie = 2 points, par = 1, bogey = 0). Great for groups with wide handicap ranges. Less psychological pressure than match play.

**Bingo Bango Bongo:** Points awarded for first on the green (bingo), closest to pin (bango), and first to hole out (bongo). Fun for slower, casual rounds.

Nassau is the default because it's easy to explain, creates three separate opportunities to win or lose, and runs cleanly from first tee to 18th green.
